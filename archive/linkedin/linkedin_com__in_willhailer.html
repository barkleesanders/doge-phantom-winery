  • Andrew Sandstrom

    Unleash Utah • 534 followers

    Congress is moving forward with the DOMINANCE Act—bipartisan legislation that reorganizes how the U.S. secures critical mineral supply chains. The bill does something practical: it aligns federal capabilities across State, Commerce, DOE, and development finance institutions to accelerate mining and processing projects with trusted allies. Instead of fragmented efforts, it creates Energy Security Compacts—a coordinated platform to support strategic mineral investments abroad and build capacity in secure markets outside of China. Beijing controls over 70% of global rare earth mining and nearly 90% of refining. That’s leverage they’ve used before, and it’s leverage we can’t afford to ignore. Here’s where Utah fits in. While the DOMINANCE Act focuses on building international capacity with allies, Utah is building domestic capacity. Our state has committed to supplying roughly 25% of U.S. critical-mineral demand, cutting permitting times in half, and processing at least half of those minerals in-state. New discoveries like Ionic Mineral Technologies’ halloysite deposits show that Utah’s geology can deliver. The strategic logic is straightforward: expand production with allies abroad, expand production at home, and build processing capacity in places with the workforce and infrastructure to support it. Utah checks those boxes. This isn’t just about minerals. It’s about whether the U.S. can manufacture semiconductors, build data centers, produce advanced batteries, and maintain defense systems without depending on adversaries. Supply chain resilience is national security. I’m encouraged to see federal policy and state action moving in the same direction. If you’re working on critical minerals projects or in the energy space, I’d welcome the chance to connect.

  • Tracy Staedter

    Sustain Rural Wisconsin… • 2K followers

    In Wisconsin, millions of dollars of public funding is financing a handful of mega-dairy, manure-to-biogas projects. The funding is coming from bond financing, a method where governments, municipalities, or corporations raise money by issuing bonds to investors. When you buy a bond, you are essentially lending money to the issuer, who promises to pay you regular interest (called the coupon) and return your original investment (the principal) at a set maturity date. This approach is commonly used to fund large projects or infrastructure, allowing issuers to access significant capital upfront while spreading repayment over time. A few days ago, Bloomberg reported a default on the very first payment for a $41.5 million tax-exempt bond issued by the Town of Gillett, WI, in 2021 to finance WI RNG Hub North, a mega-dairy biogas facility. https://lnkd.in/gyuH5Jgb The intention was to convert waste from 7,200 cows into methane gas. The deal involved complex financing with international firms and public economic development agencies. The default has left investors facing a $1.7 million principal shortfall and highlights some critical risks to bond financing. Importantly, it does not appear that community input was a formal part of the approval and financing process for WI RNG Hub North in the first place. Details are still emerging, but it's clear that when small rural towns like Gillett are asked to float millions in public bonds to support privately owned factory farms, taxpayers and local governments may bear the financial fallout. Due diligence, transparency, and community input is necessary before issuing public bonds for large-scale industrial projects. Rural communities deserve investments that protect both their economic vitality and environmental health. #BondFinancing #MunicipalBonds #PublicFinance #RuralCommunities #SustainableInvestment #MegaDairy #Biogas #Wisconsin

The District Capitalized Revolving Fund Program provides direct financial support to help small businesses grow or stabilize operations through microloans and small business loans.
